<h2>The technique</h2>

<p>Khine's "Shrinky-Dink microfluidics" technique prints microfluidic patterns onto Shrinky-Dink polymer sheets with a regular laser printer, then heats them in a toaster oven so the sheets shrink and the patterns turn into micro-channels and microwells. The whole process goes from concept to working device in minutes.</p>

<p>That single technique underpins both BBComp-traceable Khine spinoffs (Vena Vitals and Makani Science) and the broader portfolio of wearable biosensors her lab has produced.</p>

<h2>The two BBComp wins</h2>

<p>Khine mentored both top-2 placements in the 2014 Beall track, the first joint cycle in BBComp history:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>2014 Beall 1st</strong>: <a href="{{ '/years/2014/' | relative_url }}">Spero Diagnostics</a>, a sepsis diagnostic targeting under three hours and under $100 per test. Co-mentored with Prof. Zoran Nenadic.</li>
  <li><strong>2014 Beall 2nd</strong>: <a href="{{ '/years/2014/' | relative_url }}">A Hundred Tiny Hands</a>, an academic non-profit selling children's "Inventor's Toolboxes" built around lab inventions like the SuperHydro-Tension surface-tension kit and the Polytropolis electricity kit.</li>
</ul>

<p>That six-person pipeline is documented in the published Khine Lab alumni roster. As far as we have found in the BBComp archive, no other UCI faculty mentor has produced more PhD graduates from past BBComp teams.</p>

<h2>Two BBComp-traceable spinoff companies</h2>

<h3>Vena Vitals</h3>

<p>Co-founded in 2019 by Khine, her PhD student Josh Kim and outside CEO Ray Liu. Vena Vitals is the commercial follow-on of the <a href="{{ '/years/2019/' | relative_url }}">2019 Beall 1st-place team Qardiac</a>. Y Combinator Summer 2020 batch. The product is a Band-Aid-sized skin sticker that captures blood pressure continuously without a cuff. FDA clearance anticipated in 2026. New R&amp;D laboratory opened at Roseman University of Nevada Summerlin on March 25, 2026.</p>

<p>See the full <a href="{{ '/stories/#vena-vitals' | relative_url }}">Vena Vitals case study</a>.</p>

<h3>Makani Science</h3>

<p>Co-founded in 2019 by Khine and her former doctoral student Michael Chu (CTO). The product is a bandage-sized respiratory monitor patch using the same Shrinky-Dink microfluidics technique. FDA 510(k) clearance for the Makani Respiratory Monitor received April 2, 2025. $1.1 million NIH grant under the NHLBI program (HHS TAGGS award R61HL177500).</p>

<p>See the full <a href="{{ '/stories/#makani' | relative_url }}">Makani Science case study</a>.</p>

<h2>Spinoffs from before her UCI tenure</h2>

<p>Khine joined UCI in 2009. Her earlier work produced two more startups that predate her UCI Beall Butterworth Competition involvement:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Shrink Nanotechnologies</strong>, spun out of her UC Merced tenure (2006-2009). Reportedly the first startup ever to come out of UC Merced.</li>
  <li><strong>Fluxion Biosciences</strong>, San Francisco. Single-cell electroporation, from her PhD work.</li>
</ul>

<p>She also serves on the Scientific Advisory Board of <strong>Novoheart</strong>, a cardiac-modeling company.</p>

<h2>Why this is worth noting</h2>

<p>The Khine Lab line in the BBComp archive is the strongest counter-example to the "senior project that ends at graduation" story. Two of her BBComp-mentored teams turned into ongoing commercial entities. Six of her BBComp alumni are now PhDs working on related research. One of her spinoff products is FDA-cleared. The lab is a real pipeline, not a one-time mentor relationship.</p>

<p>If the BBComp archive is mostly a record of student products that did not survive graduation, the Khine Lab subset is the unusually high tail of the distribution.</p>
